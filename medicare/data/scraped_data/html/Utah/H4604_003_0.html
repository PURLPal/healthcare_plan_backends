    What's the standard Part B premium?

    This is the monthly fee you'll pay for your Part B coverage. In 2026, the standard Part B premium is $202.90 per month. This amount can change each year, and you pay it in addition to your plan's monthly premium.

    Your Part B premium could be higher than the standard amount, depending on your income. Learn how your income affects your Part B premium.

    Your Part B premium could also be lower than the standard amount if:

    • You join a Medicare Advantage Plan that offers a Part B premium reduction benefit. If your plan offers this benefit, they'll contribute an amount (which varies by plan) towards your Part B premium payment, reducing what you pay each month. This is also known as the Part B premium "giveback" or "buy down."
    • You get other help with costs.
    • You have Medicaid. (Medicaid pays the Part B premium for most people who qualify for Medicaid.)

    What's the Part B premium reduction?

    Some Medicare Advantage Plans offer a Part B premium reduction benefit. If your plan offers this benefit, theyll contribute an amount (which varies by plan) towards your Part B premium payment, reducing what you pay each month. This is also known as the Part B premium "giveback" or "buy down."

    If your plan's premium reduction won't cover the full cost of your Part B premium, you'll owe the rest of your Part B premium each month, along with your plan's monthly premium.

    Who's eligible?

    To qualify for the Part B premium reduction, you must:

    • Join a plan that offers a reduction
    • Have both Part A and Part B
    • Live in the plan's service area
    • Pay your own Part B premium (if another program is already paying your Part B premium, you won't qualify for this reduction)

    How can you get it?

    If you pay your Part B premium through Social Security: Your Part B premium reduction will be credited to your Social Security check.

    If you pay your Part B premium directly to Medicare: Your monthly bill will reflect a lower amount.

    When will you get the reduction?

    If you qualify, your monthly reduction will be automatically applied to your Social Security check or Part B premium bill. If you've recently switched plans, it may take several months for the reduction to start. Once it does, you'll be reimbursed for any months you didn't get a reduction (starting with the month you joined the plan).

    How do pharmacy networks affect what I pay?

    Medicare drug plans have contracts with pharmacies, called "networks." Pharmacies in a plan's "network" have agreed to offer the plan's members lower costs for drugs. A pharmacy's "network status" tells you if your plan has negotiated lower costs with that pharmacy. Here are the 4 "network statuses" a pharmacy can have, and how they affect your costs:

    1. Preferred in-network: Usually offers the lowest costs for drugs.
    2. In-network: Usually offers drugs for a lower cost than out-of-network pharmacies, but a higher cost than preferred in-network pharmacies.
    3. Out-of-network: Usually offers drugs at a higher cost, and you may have to pay the full cost.
    4. Mail-order: Costs vary based on the specific mail-order pharmacy. Contact the plan for more information.

    Learn more about drug tiers

    Plans place their covered drugs into different categories called "tiers" based on how much they cost.

    If your prescriber (your doctor or other health care provider who's legally allowed to write prescriptions) thinks you need a drug in a higher cost tier, you or your prescriber may be allowed to ask your plan for an exception to get a lower cost for that drug. For more information on exceptions, visit Medicare.gov/medicare-prescription-drug-coverage-appeals

    Note: Plans can change their list of covered drugs (also called a "formulary") at any time, including moving covered drugs to a different tier. Your plan will notify you of any formulary changes that affect drugs youre taking.
